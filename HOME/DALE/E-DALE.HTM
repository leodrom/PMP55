 A little about myself

If you've stumbled onto this page and have a few minutes to spare, let's get acquainted.

You've probably guessed it already (let's assume you can read and made it through the first line), my name is Dmytro Zlatous—first name Dmytro, last name Zlatous, spelled without the final "t". I'm not related to John Chrysostom.

I was born on 20 September 1976 — a Monday, naturally — in Ivano-Frankivsk where my parents were diligently chiseling away at the granite of science.

As a child I was hot-tempered, leaving my parents cold with shock. I'd manage to soak or, even better, completely ruin what grown-ups called a diaper, or I'd start singing from boredom (usually at night to enjoythe silence around me). There were plenty of other pursuits but let's skip them; I'm sure you had just as many ways to give your parents joy.

So, if I haven't exhausted you yet, let's continue. After the exhausting moves (Ivano-Frankivsk – Radomyshl – Kyiv) I ended up in a plain provincial town called Stryi. There I attended a wonderful kindergarten with the fairy-tale name "Red Riding Hood" (guess who played the "hoods" and who the wolves were).

When I was old enough I shouldered my bag and set off for secondary school No. 7. Whenever I actually made it to class my knowledge grew by leaps and bounds. The subjects I remember best were:

    Algebra – all attempts to master long division failed miserably, so I concluded the calculator is man's best friend.

    Geometry – all my teacher's attempts to make me draw with a ruler and pencil came to nothing.

    Computer science – fascinating digits and letters glowing on that TV/ typewriter hybrid.

    History – the textbook was packed with killer illustrations.

    Russian literature – out of principle I wouldn't read the assigned works—I hate being told what to do.

    Chemistry – always wondered if my neighbour's ruler would dissolve completely in sulfuric acid.

    Physics – a charged capacitor left on the table made a great "gin and tonic" for curious types.

    English – I could never make out what that "cutie" was babbling or why she looked at me with such loving eyes.

    Geography – like Ostap Bender I wondered where the country was where everyone wore white pants (Rio de Janeiro).

As you can see, a whole range of subjects fascinated me for very different reasons.

When I decided I was done with secondary education (usually around 16), I had to pick a path. Because school never taught me to count properly and those mysterious symbols on the "hybrid" kept calling, I chose applied mathematics—hoping that by my fifth year I'd finally master counting.

So I found myself at the mystifying Faculty of Applied Mathematics and Informatics, specifically in the Information Systems department. The fifth year is almost over and I still can't count without a calculator. Alas!
