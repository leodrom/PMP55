 A little about myself

If by some mysterious way you've found this page and aren't rushing off for the next few minutes, let's get acquainted.

As you've probably already guessed (let's assume you can read and managed to get through the first line here) my name is Dmytro Zlatous (Dmytro is the first name, Zlatous the surname—spelled without the ending "t"; I'm not claiming any relation to John Chrysostom).

I was born on September 20, 1976 (as expected, it was a Monday) in the city of Ivano-Frankivsk where my parents diligently mined the granite of science.

In my childhood my hot temper showed itself, so my parents were literally chilled by their offspring. I'd manage to soak or, even better, completely soil what adults called a "diaper," or I'd start singing from boredom (usually at night to enjoymy talent in the surrounding silence). Of course there were plenty of other great activities but let's not dwell—I'm sure you had no fewer tricks to make your parents happy.

So, if I haven't bored you yet, let's continue. After all the tiring moves (Ivano-Frankivsk – Radomyshl – Kyiv) I had to settle in a plain provincial town called Stryi. There I went to a cool kindergarten with the fairy-tale name "Red Riding Hood" (obviously the "hoods" were played by the kind teachers, and the "wolves" you can guess yourself).

When age allowed I took my pack and went off to gain wisdom at secondary school No. 7. During those moments when I managed to make it to class my knowledge grew by leaps and bounds. The subjects I remember most were:

    Algebra – all my attempts to master long division failed miserably, so I realised a calculator is man's best friend.

    Geometry – all the teacher's efforts to make me draw with a ruler and pencil were fruitless.

    Computer science – those curious digits and letters flickered on that TV/typewriter hybrid.

    History – the whole textbook was crammed with awesome pictures.

    Russian literature – on principle I refused to read the assigned works (I hate being told what and when to do).

    Chemistry – always wondered if my neighbour's ruler would dissolve completely in sulfuric acid and what would happen if you "drop some of that" here.

    Physics – a charged capacitor left on the desk was great "gin and tonic" for the curious.

    English – I could never understand what that "cutie" was babbling or why she looked at me with such loving eyes.

    Geography – like Ostap Bender I wanted to know where the country is where everyone wears white pants (Rio de Janeiro).

As you can see a whole range of subjects fascinated me for very different reasons.

When I decided I was done with secondary education (usually around 16) I had to choose a path. Since school never taught me to count properly and the mysterious symbols on that "hybrid" kept luring me, I decided: I'll study applied mathematics—perhaps by my fifth year I'll master that trickery (meaning I'll finally learn to count).

And so I landed at the mysterious Faculty of Applied Mathematics and Informatics where fate placed me in the Information Systems department. Now the fifth year is nearly over and I still can't count without a calculator. Alas!
