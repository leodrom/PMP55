Well, a little about myself

    I was born on a cold winter day—of course I don't remember it—but it happened on January 7, 1976 AD. If those numbers make you wonder — yes, that's Christmas. The years passed in Lviv (a city in western Ukraine). I learned to read, write and do other clever things at secondary school 31. It's already 1997 and knowledge keeps filling my head; more than that, I'm finishing my studies at the Lviv State University, Faculty of Applied Mathematics (that's what the mysterious mark PMP-55 means).
    Let's reason logically. I study applied mathematics, I have the knowledge gained at school, you are reading this page — logically you learn about me while I share that knowledge. It's no wonder I can be called a provider of myself. But why be surprised? Internet pages exist precisely for this.
    Through sweat and blood, often short on sleep (not to mention physical, moral and spiritual food), I spent my free time at the computer (that thing you connect to a "television," the one you're looking at now, I hope with a smile). Working with it I concluded: it's not only a typewriter but also a tool for making money, and on the Internet scale — a source of interesting acquaintances.

The project I'm currently working on

Let's start with the problem.
    Imagine a situation where you have a text file containing information you need. No matter how useful it is, you can work with it only if it's presented in a more convenient form (lists, trees and so on). To do that you have to write bulky code that correctly reads the text file and puts the data into some structure. If you've faced this problem you couldn't help but notice that reading data raises another issue: who will check its correctness? Anyone with at least some notion of how compilers are built will immediately think of sketching the syntax structure before starting on the reader.

What we came up with.
    You're facing the above problem. You haven't written a PhD thesis on building compilers and don't know how to approach it. We came up with this idea. If you fully understand the structure of the data you process, you can represent it with syntax diagrams.
    Our program includes an editor for visually building syntax diagrams. In our opinion it's very handy. For example, the diagrams are "drawn" faster than you would on a sheet of paper.
    One advantage of this project is that it's written as a component for Borland Delphi. This allows you to plug it into your own software products.
    We hope this component will be used not only by us but also by those who spend a lot of time solving this issue.
